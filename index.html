Fertility Rate

The fertility rate is the average number of births per woman, over her lifetime.

The declining fertility rate is due to factors such as:

  • Better access to contraception
  • Greater opportunities for women
  • Improved healthcare

Declining fertility rates therefore indicate improved socioeconomic conditions for many countries.

Currently, about two-thirds of the world's population is below the critical 2.1 threshold, which allows one generation to exactly replace itself.

Most notably, Europe and Central Asia as well as North America have seen persistently low fertility rates since the 60s. In contrast, Sub-Saharan Africa has consistently seen high fertility rates.

Sub-Saharan Africa is home to the top 15 highest fertility rates in the world with the country of Niger taking out the top spot with a fertility rate of 6.82 as of 2021.

And what about Australia?

Australia's current fertility rate is 1.70, well below the 2.1 critical threshold. It is a reflection of how Australian women are gradually having children later in life, and having fewer children when they do.

Currently Australia's population is 25.69 million. Under the low fertility scenario, the population is projected to be 36.2 million in 2060-61. Additionally, deaths will exceed births from 2048-49.
